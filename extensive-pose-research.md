# Human Pose Estimation: Theory, Open-Source Landscape, and a Recommendation for Edge Fall Detection

## TL;DR
- **Pose estimation** converts images into skeletons of labeled keypoints; the field divides along two axes — top-down vs. bottom-up (how multiple people are handled) and heatmap vs. regression/coordinate-classification (how a single keypoint is localized) — and understanding those two axes explains almost every model you will encounter.
- The open-source landscape in 2025-2026 is dominated by permissively-licensed, real-time families (RTMPose/RTMO under Apache-2.0, MediaPipe/BlazePose, MoveNet), accuracy leaders (ViTPose, Sapiens, HRNet), and the AGPL-3.0 Ultralytics YOLO-pose line the existing system already uses; OpenPose remains the historical reference but is encumbered by a restrictive non-commercial CMU license.
- **Recommendation: reimplement RTMPose (specifically RTMPose-s/m, the SimCC coordinate-classification top-down model).** It is the best single choice for learning modern pose theory deeply while staying directly relevant to a real-time, edge-deployable fall detector: it is Apache-2.0, has a clear paper plus reference code in MMPose, teaches the important post-heatmap paradigm (coordinate classification), and runs fast on CPU/mobile/Jetson-class hardware. Fall-detection robustness (the bending false-positive problem) is best fixed with temporal logic on top of keypoints rather than by swapping the pose model.

## Key Findings

1. **Two orthogonal design choices define the field.** *Top-down* (detect people, then estimate each person's pose) gives the best per-person accuracy but scales in cost with the number of people; *bottom-up* (detect all keypoints, then group) has cost roughly independent of crowd size but historically lower accuracy. Separately, *heatmap* methods predict a Gaussian probability map per keypoint (accurate, but quantization error and compute-heavy), while *regression* / *coordinate-classification* methods output coordinates directly (fast, deploy-friendly, historically less accurate until RLE and SimCC closed the gap).

2. **For a single elderly farmer working alone, the crowd-density advantage of bottom-up is irrelevant** — the scene is one or few persons. That argues for a top-down or single-stage model optimized for latency, exactly the RTMPose/YOLO-pose/MoveNet niche.

3. **The current YOLOv8-pose system is a reasonable production choice but AGPL-3.0.** For a learning-oriented reimplementation with a cleaner license and more instructive theory, RTMPose (Apache-2.0, SimCC) is superior; RTMPose also outperforms YOLO-pose-style single-stage models per-person while remaining real-time.

4. **The bending false-positive problem is a fall-logic problem, not a keypoint-accuracy problem.** The literature repeatedly shows single-frame geometric heuristics (aspect ratio, torso collapse) fire on bending/crouching; the fixes are temporal (multi-frame buffers, velocity of hip descent, sustained-pose confirmation, angle trajectories).

## Details

### PART 1 — Theory

**What 2D pose estimation is.** Human pose estimation (HPE) localizes anatomical keypoints (joints/landmarks such as nose, shoulders, elbows, wrists, hips, knees, ankles) in an image and connects them into a skeleton. The 2D task outputs pixel coordinates (x, y), usually with a visibility/confidence value per keypoint. It is the foundation for action recognition, human-computer interaction, and fall detection.

**Keypoint formats.** The dominant standard is **COCO's 17-keypoint** topology (nose, 2 eyes, 2 ears, 2 shoulders, 2 elbows, 2 wrists, 2 hips, 2 knees, 2 ankles); the MS COCO keypoints dataset (Lin et al., 2014) has over 200,000 images and 250,000 person instances, each labeled with 17 keypoints (train2017 ≈ 57K images/150K instances; val2017 = 5,000 images). **MPII uses 16 keypoints** (built around head top, neck, and a pelvis/thorax spine, with the classic PCKh evaluation). **BlazePose/MediaPipe uses 33 keypoints**, a superset of COCO adding hands, feet, and face landmarks for scale/orientation, aimed at fitness/AR. Other topologies include AI Challenger (14 keypoints), CrowdPose (14 keypoints), COCO-WholeBody (133 keypoints including face/hands/feet), and research schemes such as Halpe (up to 136) and Sapiens (up to 308 keypoints).

**Top-down vs. bottom-up.** Top-down methods (HRNet, SimpleBaseline, RTMPose, AlphaPose) first run a person detector, crop each person, and estimate keypoints on the person-centric patch — high accuracy because the model works at full per-person resolution, but compute grows linearly with the number of people, and the pose is only as good as the detector's box. Bottom-up methods (OpenPose, HigherHRNet, associative-embedding models) detect all keypoints in the whole image at once and then group them into individuals; cost is roughly constant regardless of crowd size, which is efficient for crowds, but per-person accuracy is typically lower (chiefly due to lower per-person resolution) and grouping is hard when people overlap. The OpenPose authors note that the top-down accuracy advantage stems from feeding each cropped person at higher resolution, whereas bottom-up feeds the whole image at smaller per-person resolution.

**Heatmap vs. regression, and quantization error.** Heatmap methods predict, for each keypoint, a 2D map whose peak is the predicted location; they are supervised densely (stable training) and dominate accuracy benchmarks. Their intrinsic drawbacks: (1) the heatmap resolution is lower than the input due to downsampling, causing **quantization error** when you take the argmax — the true subpixel location is lost; (2) the argmax/"taking-maximum" decoding step is non-differentiable, preventing fully end-to-end training; and (3) high-resolution heatmaps are compute- and memory-expensive, especially in 3D. Quantization error is "significantly exacerbated as the size of the input image decreases," so heatmaps are barely better than regression at low resolution — a key fact for edge devices. Mitigations include the "shift a quarter toward the second-max" trick, DARK (distribution-aware decoding), and offset/UDP methods. Regression methods output coordinates directly via fully-connected layers; they are fast, end-to-end, and free of quantization error, but were historically less accurate because dense supervision is easier to learn than a single coordinate.

**Bridging paradigms.** *Integral/soft-argmax regression* (Sun et al., ECCV 2018) computes the expectation over the heatmap, unifying heatmap and regression and removing the non-differentiable argmax. *RLE (Residual Log-likelihood Estimation, Li et al., ICCV 2021 Oral, arXiv:2107.11291)* reframes regression through maximum-likelihood estimation with a normalizing-flow model that learns the *residual* of the output distribution rather than the raw distribution; "regression with RLE bring 12.4 mAP improvement on MSCOCO without any test-time overhead. Moreover, for the first time, especially on multi-person pose estimation, our regression method is superior to the heatmap-based methods" (71.3 mAP at 4.0 GFLOPs vs. 71.0 mAP at 9.7 GFLOPs for a comparable heatmap model). *SimCC (Li et al., ECCV 2022 Oral, arXiv:2107.03332)* reframes localization as **two 1-D classification problems** — one for the x-axis, one for the y-axis — dividing each axis into fine numbered bins so localization reaches subpixel precision without expensive upsampling; it outperforms heatmaps especially at low resolution and uses just two fully-connected layers, making it very deployment-friendly.

**Key architectures.**
- **OpenPose / Part Affinity Fields (Cao et al., CVPR 2017 / TPAMI 2019, arXiv:1812.08008):** the seminal bottom-up method. A VGG-19 stem (first 10 layers) feeds a multi-stage CNN that jointly predicts *part confidence maps* (keypoint heatmaps) and *Part Affinity Fields (PAFs)* — 2D vector fields encoding limb location and orientation — then a greedy bipartite matching links keypoints into people. Realtime and crowd-invariant; first open-source system to jointly do body+foot+face+hand (up to 135 keypoints). The TPAMI version showed PAF-only refinement (vs. refining both PAFs and confidence maps) improves both speed and accuracy.
- **Stacked Hourglass (Newell et al., ECCV 2016, arXiv:1603.06937):** stacks encoder-decoder ("hourglass") modules built from residual blocks that repeatedly pool down and upsample back up (nearest-neighbor upsampling + elementwise addition of skip connections), with *intermediate supervision* at each stack. Repeated bottom-up/top-down processing captures multi-scale spatial relationships; heatmap output. Set the template for many later designs; SOTA on FLIC and MPII at release.
- **SimpleBaseline (Xiao et al., ECCV 2018):** deliberately minimal — a ResNet backbone plus three *deconvolution* layers to produce heatmaps. Demonstrated that a plain design reaches ~70.4 AP (ResNet-50, 256×192) and is a clean, instructive baseline; still widely used as the reference top-down pipeline.
- **HRNet (Sun et al., CVPR 2019):** maintains *high-resolution representations throughout* the network via parallel multi-resolution branches with repeated cross-resolution fusion, rather than recovering resolution from a low-res bottleneck. HRNet-W32 reaches ~73.4-75.1 AP at 256×192 and became the dominant top-down backbone; nearly all 2019 COCO keypoint challenge entrants used HRNet.
- **Associative Embedding (Newell et al.):** a bottom-up grouping method that predicts, alongside each keypoint heatmap, a "tag" embedding so that keypoints with similar tags are grouped into the same person — an alternative to PAFs (HigherHRNet built on this with ~67-68 AP bottom-up on COCO val).

**Newer paradigms.**
- **YOLO-pose (single-stage):** the Ultralytics YOLOv8-pose / YOLO11-pose and community YOLOv7-pose add a keypoint-regression head to a YOLO detector, predicting boxes and 17 COCO keypoints in one pass — fast, end-to-end, and edge-friendly.
- **RTMPose (SimCC-based, top-down):** discussed in the recommendation below.
- **RTMO (Lu et al., CVPR 2024, arXiv:2312.07526):** a one-stage model that embeds coordinate classification (dual 1-D heatmaps) into a YOLO architecture with a Dynamic Coordinate Classifier (bins dynamically allocated to a region 1.25× the predicted box) and an MLE-based loss. **RTMO-l attains 74.8% AP on COCO val2017 at 141 FPS on a single V100**, matching top-down accuracy without a separate detector and with inference time independent of the number of people (per-variant COCO test-dev: RTMO-s 66.9/67.7†, -m 70.1/71.5†, -l 71.6/73.3†; † = extra training data).
- **Transformer-based:** *TokenPose* represents each keypoint as a token and learns constraints via attention; *TransPose* uses a transformer as a heatmap decoder; *PETR* does end-to-end multi-person pose as set prediction (DETR-style). *ViTPose (Xu et al., NeurIPS 2022, arXiv:2204.12484)* uses a plain, non-hierarchical ViT backbone with a lightweight decoder, scales from 100M to 1B parameters, and its largest model (ViTPose-G with ViTAE-G, 1B params) reached **80.9 AP on COCO test-dev** (ensemble 81.1) — the accuracy state of the art. *Sapiens (Khirodkar et al., Meta, ECCV 2024 Oral, arXiv:2408.12569)* pretrains ViTs on 300M human images and supports native 1K-resolution inference across pose/segmentation/depth/normal, improving pose SOTA on its Humans-5K benchmark by 7.6 mAP; models scale 0.3B-2B parameters.

**3D pose estimation.** Monocular 3D adds a depth (z) coordinate. The dominant, modular approach is **2D-to-3D lifting**: run a 2D estimator, then a second network lifts the 2D skeleton to 3D. *VideoPose3D (Pavllo et al., CVPR 2019)* lifts a sequence of 2D keypoints to 3D using **dilated temporal convolutions** over a 243-frame receptive field plus semi-supervised back-projection, reaching **~46.8 mm MPJPE on Human3.6M** (Protocol 1, with fine-tuned CPN 2D detections). Accuracy of lifting is bounded by the 2D detector quality. Multi-person 3D and parametric-body approaches (SMPL-based) exist but are heavier; BlazePose GHUM also emits relative 3D per frame for on-device use.

**Evaluation metrics.**
- **OKS (Object Keypoint Similarity):** the IoU-analog for keypoints on COCO. OKS = Σ exp(−dᵢ²/(2s²kᵢ²))·δ(vᵢ>0) / Σ δ(vᵢ>0), where dᵢ is the Euclidean distance between predicted and ground-truth keypoint, s is the object scale, kᵢ is a per-keypoint falloff constant (kᵢ = 2σᵢ), and vᵢ is the visibility flag. It normalizes for object size and per-joint annotation difficulty.
- **AP/mAP on COCO:** average precision computed over OKS thresholds 0.50:0.05:0.95 (primary metric), plus AP⁵⁰, AP⁷⁵, and scale-specific APᴹ/APᴸ, and AR.
- **PCK / PCKh:** Percentage of Correct Keypoints — a joint is correct if within a distance threshold; **PCKh@0.5** (threshold = 50% of head-segment length) is the MPII standard.
- **MPJPE:** Mean Per Joint Position Error (mm), the standard 3D metric; **PA-MPJPE** applies a rigid (Procrustes) alignment first.

**Standard datasets.** *COCO keypoints* — 200K+ images, 250K+ instances, 17 keypoints, in-the-wild, OKS/AP evaluation; the de facto benchmark. *MPII* — ~25K images, ~40K people, 16 keypoints, PCKh evaluation, richly articulated everyday activities. *CrowdPose* — ~20K images, ~80K instances, 14 keypoints, deliberately crowded/occluded scenes, adds APᴱ/APᴹ/APᴴ (easy/medium/hard by crowd index) to stress-test grouping and occlusion.

### PART 2 — Open-Source Implementation Landscape

**OpenPose (CMU).** Classic bottom-up PAF system (Caffe; C++/Python). Body/foot/face/hand, up to 135 keypoints; runtime roughly constant in the number of people. Accuracy roughly ~80% AP-class on COCO in its original configuration; runs ~8-22 FPS on a TITAN X depending on resolution. **License is the critical issue: CMU non-commercial only** — free for academic/non-profit research, but commercial use requires a separately negotiated license. Per CMU's commercial terms (FlintBox): "The non-exclusive commercial license requires a non-refundable USD 25,000 annual royalty. The non-exclusive commercial license cannot be used in the field of Sports." Still maintained as the literature's baseline, but do not ship it commercially without a CMU agreement. Not a good reimplementation target for edge/commercial work.

**MMPose (OpenMMLab).** The large PyTorch toolbox; **Apache-2.0** ("This project is released under the Apache 2.0 license"). Implements essentially the whole field — SimpleBaseline, HRNet, Hourglass, associative embedding, RLE, SimCC, RTMPose, RTMO, ViTPose, DEKR, 2D/3D and whole-body — with a unified config system and MMDeploy export to ONNX Runtime, TensorRT, ncnn, OpenVINO, RKNN, PPLNN, and NVIDIA Jetson. Best single hub for study and for reference implementations.

**RTMPose / RTMO (MMPose ecosystem).** RTMPose is a top-down SimCC-based real-time family (Apache-2.0, PyTorch). Per the paper (Jiang et al., arXiv:2303.07399): "Our RTMPose-m achieves 75.8% AP on COCO with 90+ FPS on an Intel i7-11700 CPU and 430+ FPS on an NVIDIA GTX 1660 Ti GPU," and RTMPose-s "achieves 72.2% AP on COCO with 70+ FPS on a Snapdragon 865 chip." Using RTMDet-nano as the pipeline detector, "RTMPose-m can achieve 73.2% AP." Variants (256×192, AIC+COCO pretrain, per GitHub model zoo): RTMPose-t 68.5 AP / 3.34M params / 0.36 GFLOPs; -s 72.2 / 5.47M / 0.68; -m 75.8 / 13.59M / 1.93; -l 76.5 / 27.66M / 4.16. Architecture: CSPNeXt backbone + a SimCC head (a convolutional layer, a fully-connected layer, and a Gated Attention Unit that refines K keypoint representations) that treats 2D localization as two 1-D coordinate-classification tasks; micro-designs include a 7×7 final conv and a SORD-style soft-label loss. RTMO is the one-stage sibling (see above). Explicitly designed for CPU/GPU/Jetson/mobile deployment. The single best fit for an edge fall-detection reimplementation.

**YOLO-pose family (Ultralytics).** YOLOv8-pose, YOLO11-pose (and community YOLOv7-pose). Single-stage, 17 COCO keypoints, anchor-free head, extremely easy to use (`pip install ultralytics`), PyTorch, exports to ONNX/TensorRT/CoreML/TFLite; broadly deployed on Jetson and Pi-class devices. **License is AGPL-3.0** (or a paid Ultralytics Enterprise license) — the network-copyleft AGPL obliges you to release source of derivative networked services, which is why the license matters for a product. This is what the current system uses; it is a fine production choice but a less clean license and less theoretically novel target than RTMPose.

**MediaPipe Pose / BlazePose (Google, arXiv:2006.10204).** On-device, lightweight, **33 keypoints (2D and relative 3D)**, detector-tracker pipeline (detect once, then track from previous-frame ROI), uses both heatmaps (training) and regression (inference). Runs 30+ FPS on a Pixel-2-class CPU, super-real-time on GPU. Apache-2.0. Single-person-oriented (best for one prominent subject) — a strong practical option for a single-farmer scene, though multi-person handling is weaker. Reference model weights are released but the full training pipeline is not open.

**MoveNet (TensorFlow, Lightning & Thunder).** Ultra-fast 17-keypoint model; **Lightning** (192×192 input, latency-critical) and **Thunder** (256×256, higher accuracy). Both run 30+ FPS on modern phones/laptops; TF.js/TFLite (int8/fp16) for browser and edge; there is also a MultiPose Lightning variant whose speed is independent of the number of people. Apache-2.0. Excellent edge deployability; weights are open but training code/paper detail is limited, making it a weaker *reimplementation* target than RTMPose.

**HRNet & variants.** The reference high-accuracy top-down backbone (PyTorch). HRNet-W32/W48 ~74-77 AP; heavier than real-time on edge. Lightweight spinoffs (Lite-HRNet, Dite-HRNet, EL-HRNet) push params down (e.g., EL-HRNet ~5M params / 2.0 GFLOPs, 67.1 AP on COCO2017 val). Instructive for the high-resolution idea but compute-heavy in original form.

**ViTPose / ViTPose++.** Transformer accuracy leaders (Apache-2.0, PyTorch/MMPose). ViTPose-G ~80.9 AP COCO test-dev; ViTPose++ adds mixture-of-experts for body/animal/whole-body across COCO, COCO-WholeBody, MPII, AIC, and AP-10K. Large and GPU-hungry — great for understanding transformers in pose, impractical to run on Jetson-Nano-class hardware at real time.

**AlphaPose (SJTU).** Top-down (RMPE — Regional Multi-Person Pose Estimation), whole-body up to 136 (Halpe) keypoints, symmetric integral keypoint regression for continuous joint prediction. Strong accuracy; PyTorch. License includes non-commercial restrictions (research use), so check terms before commercial use.

**DWPose.** A distillation-improved whole-body model (RTMPose-based, two-stage distillation) widely used to drive ControlNet/whole-body applications; MMPose-compatible.

**Sapiens (Meta).** ViT foundation models for human vision (pose/seg/depth/normal), ECCV 2024 Oral; 0.3B-2B params, native 1K-resolution, pretrained on 300M human images. Accuracy SOTA on human-centric tasks but far too heavy for edge real time; notable as a research direction.

### PART 3 — Recommendation

**Reimplement RTMPose (target the -s and -m variants, top-down SimCC coordinate classification).**

**Why RTMPose over the alternatives:**

- **Theory-learning value (high).** Reimplementing RTMPose forces you to build the whole modern top-down pipeline and, crucially, the *post-heatmap* localization paradigm that is where the field has moved. You implement: (1) a person-detection front end, (2) a CSPNeXt backbone, (3) the SimCC head — a convolution + fully-connected layer + a Gated Attention Unit (GAU) — and (4) coordinate classification, in which each axis is discretized into fine numbered bins and keypoint localization becomes two 1-D classification problems reaching subpixel precision. Along the way you directly confront the heatmap-vs-regression tradeoff, quantization error, and why classification-over-bins sidesteps it. This teaches more contemporary theory than reimplementing a pure heatmap classic.
- **Accessible published details.** There is a clear arXiv paper (2303.07399) with an explicit ablation "roadmap," and Apache-2.0 reference code plus configs and pretrained weights in MMPose. SimCC (ECCV 2022) and the GAU are separately documented, so every component is traceable.
- **License (clean).** RTMPose and MMPose are **Apache-2.0** — permissive, commercial-friendly, no copyleft. This is a concrete advantage over the current AGPL-3.0 YOLOv8-pose and over OpenPose/AlphaPose's non-commercial terms, and it matters if the greenhouse system ever becomes a product.
- **Edge / real-time suitability (excellent).** RTMPose was engineered for deployment: RTMPose-s (5.47M params, 0.68 GFLOPs, 72.2 AP) and RTMPose-t (3.34M, 0.36 GFLOPs, 68.5 AP) are tiny; MMDeploy exports to TensorRT, ONNX Runtime, ncnn, and NVIDIA Jetson. On a GTX 1660 Ti, TensorRT-FP16 pose latency is ~1.4 ms (-s) to ~2.3 ms (-m), and RTMPose-s hits 70+ FPS on a Snapdragon 865 phone — comfortably real-time on Jetson Orin Nano-class hardware, and the tiny variant is a candidate even for a Raspberry Pi with an accelerator.
- **Fit with fall detection (strong).** A single elderly farmer is a single-person, latency-sensitive scene. Top-down RTMPose gives clean, high-accuracy per-person 17-keypoint COCO skeletons — the same 17-keypoint format the current fall logic already consumes — so it is close to a drop-in upgrade for the pose stage while giving you full control of the model.

**What replicating it would involve (high level):**
1. **Data/format.** Use COCO keypoints (17-keypoint) for training/eval; adopt OKS-based AP as the metric. Optionally add a fall-specific validation set.
2. **Detector + crop.** Reuse an existing person detector (or RTMDet) to produce boxes; implement the affine crop/resize to 256×192 with aspect-ratio padding.
3. **Backbone.** Implement or import CSPNeXt; start from the small variant.
4. **SimCC head + GAU.** Implement the convolution → FC → GAU head and the two 1-D classification outputs over x and y bins; implement the SORD-style soft-label loss (softmax with temperature τ=0.1) and decode by taking the argmax bin (subpixel via bin width).
5. **Training tricks.** Reproduce the RTMDet-style training schedule (the paper's roadmap shows these tricks matter as much as architecture): data augmentation, cosine schedule, EMA, etc.
6. **Deployment.** Export to ONNX → TensorRT for Jetson; verify latency and AP parity with the reference.
7. **Fall logic (separate, and where the real accuracy win is).** Keep the pose model and the fall classifier decoupled. Replace single-frame heuristics with **temporal** logic: track hip/torso keypoint trajectories, compute velocity of hip/head descent, use torso-vertical angle over a multi-frame buffer, and require a *sustained* prone/collapsed pose plus a *motion-drop* before alerting. The literature shows bending/crouching false positives come precisely from single-frame aspect-ratio/torso-height rules and are reduced by frame buffers, weighted voting, and angle-trajectory analysis.

**Sensible fallbacks.** If you want the *simplest possible* instructive reimplementation first, build **SimpleBaseline** (ResNet + 3 deconvolutions → heatmaps) as a warm-up — it is the cleanest heatmap top-down model and makes the heatmap paradigm concrete before you tackle SimCC. If you want the *least engineering effort to a deployed edge model* rather than a learning exercise, use **MediaPipe Pose** or keep **YOLOv8-pose** (accepting AGPL) off-the-shelf. But as the single best balance of theory value + license + edge fit + application relevance, **RTMPose is the pick.**

## Recommendations

1. **Stage 1 (learn the paradigm): reimplement SimpleBaseline on COCO** to internalize heatmaps, deconvolution decoding, OKS/AP evaluation, and the top-down crop pipeline. Benchmark: reach ~70 AP (ResNet-50, 256×192).
2. **Stage 2 (the main target): reimplement RTMPose-s** with CSPNeXt + SimCC + GAU under Apache-2.0, validating against the MMPose reference. Benchmark: within ~1-2 AP of the published 72.2 AP; TensorRT export running real-time (>30 FPS) on the target Jetson.
3. **Stage 3 (application): rebuild the fall logic as a temporal module** on top of the 17-keypoint output — hip/head vertical velocity, torso angle over a rolling buffer, and sustained-pose + motion-drop confirmation. Benchmark: measure false-positive rate specifically on bending/crouching/sitting clips; target a large reduction vs. the current aspect-ratio rule.
4. **Model-size decision rule:** if the Jetson Orin Nano sustains >30 FPS for two camera feeds with RTMPose-m, use -m for accuracy; if not, drop to -s or -t. If you must run on a Raspberry-Pi-class device without an accelerator, prefer MoveNet Lightning or MediaPipe Pose over any top-down model.
5. **Thresholds that would change the recommendation:** if the deployment scene becomes genuinely crowded (multiple workers overlapping), switch to a one-stage/bottom-up model (RTMO or YOLO-pose) whose cost is crowd-independent. If commercial licensing is never a concern and you want minimal effort, staying on YOLOv8-pose is defensible.

## Caveats
- **Accuracy numbers are configuration-dependent.** RTMPose AP differs between the arXiv paper (no flip test, ImageNet or AIC+COCO pretrain) and the GitHub README headline table (AIC+COCO pretrain, with flip test); the 75.8 AP for RTMPose-m is the README/abstract figure. Per-variant parameter counts come from the MMPose model zoo, not a single paper table.
- **No official RTMPose Jetson FPS benchmark exists** in the paper or README — only Intel i7-11700 CPU, GTX 1660 Ti GPU, and Snapdragon 865 mobile numbers are published. Jetson is listed as a supported platform; expect to measure real device FPS yourself. Any Jetson RTMPose FPS figure circulating online is third-party.
- **RTMO's headline 74.8% AP is on COCO val2017; its per-variant table numbers are on COCO test-dev** (RTMO-l 73.3% with extra data) — different splits, not contradictory.
- **Vendor/blog performance claims (FPS, "faster than real-time")** should be treated as best-case; real edge throughput depends on input resolution, detector cost, batch size, quantization (int8/fp16), and the two-camera/two-model load described in the system context.
- **Fall-detection accuracy is dominated by the temporal logic and the evaluation data, not the pose backbone.** Reported 97-99% fall-detection figures in the literature come from specific, often staged datasets and do not transfer directly to a greenhouse with novel occlusions, lighting, and postures (bending over crops).
- **Licensing is summarized from project docs and should be re-verified before any commercial deployment**, as terms change (OpenPose non-commercial/CMU; Ultralytics AGPL-3.0 or Enterprise; RTMPose/MMPose/ViTPose/MediaPipe/MoveNet Apache-2.0; AlphaPose research/non-commercial).
